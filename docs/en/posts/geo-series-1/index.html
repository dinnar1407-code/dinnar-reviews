1. The “Brand Invisibility” Experiment in the Age of AI#

I ran an experiment using an AI Agent I built myself.

I fed the same set of keywords — “EV thermal management system suppliers,” “Chinese precision machining manufacturers,” “scroll compressor components” — into ChatGPT, Claude, Kimi, and Tongyi Qianwen simultaneously. The results were unsettling: the top five recommendations were either American players like Hanon, Denso, and Sanden, or emerging manufacturers from India and Vietnam.

Chinese suppliers were virtually non-existent in the AI responses.

Not because these brands lack capability — but because they haven’t left a strong enough signal in the training data and citation sources that AI models rely on. There’s a term for this phenomenon: GEO (Generative Engine Optimization).

3. GEO 1.0 → GEO 2.0#

Many people assume GEO is simply porting SEO keyword strategies onto AI platforms. That’s the most common misconception.

GEO 1.0 (2023-2025)               GEO 2.0 (2026)
─────────────────────               ─────────────────────
Keyword stuffing                    Structured data
AI-friendly article mass production Semantic alignment
Mass distribution to social media   Trust signal building
Unstable results                    Sustainable AI visibility
Easily wiped out by algorithm updates Built on genuine authority

The core logic of GEO 2.0: AI doesn’t “search” for keywords — it “understands” where your brand genuinely stands in the industry. To be cited by AI, you must first be cited by others.

I set up my OpenClaw Agent to query five AI platforms daily (ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, Kimi, Tongyi Qianwen) and analyze which brands they repeatedly recommended. Those brands shared three traits:

  1. ✅ Cited by authoritative sources (industry reports, research papers, reputable media)
  2. ✅ Complete structured data (clean Schema.org markup on their official sites)
  3. ✅ Multi-platform presence (LinkedIn, dedicated websites, technical blogs, vertical communities)
